[07:01 KST] Dynamic AMM fees as compensation for adverse selection, not a cure

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23070v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: microstructure, execution, crypto-structure
  • Trading Relevance: 8
  • Time Horizon: cycle
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: liquidity, execution, regime
  • Why it matters: Concentrated-liquidity AMMs look capital efficient on screen, but LPs are selling optionality to latency-sensitive arbitrageurs, routers, MEV searchers, and stale-price takers. The paper’s most useful finding is not that dynamic fees remove LVR, but that they mainly compensate LPs for adverse selection when stale-price risk rises. For Henry, this translates directly to a rule: visible liquidity is not usable liquidity unless fee, latency, and toxic-flow compensation are adequate. It also warns against treating DeFi depth as equivalent to centralized order book depth during volatility spikes.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: The paper models a Uniswap v3 pool with discrete block propagation, mempool latency, arbitrageurs, smart routers, MEV searchers, and active LPs. It finds that dynamic fee schedules driven by volatility and order-flow toxicity can improve hedged LP profitability mainly by increasing fee income in stale-price-risk states, while aggregate results support compensation for LVR more directly than reduction of LVR itself.
  • Cold read: The structure says market design often shifts losses into a pricing layer instead of eliminating them. In stressed crypto microstructure, the question is not “how deep is the pool” but “who is paid enough to stand there when informed flow arrives.”
  • Trading insight: For execution, avoid assuming that low displayed spread or deep TVL implies low implementation shortfall. If Henry routes size through AMM-like venues or wrapper products, require toxicity-adjusted cost, expected rebalancing loss, and latency exposure before sizing.
  • Change sensor: Dynamic fee adoption in Uniswap hooks; LVR estimates during high volatility; MEV and arbitrage latency around major BTC and ETH moves.
  • Follow-up question: Can an execution pre-check estimate “fee compensation versus toxicity” for each venue before placing size?

[07:01 KST] Ethereum scaling lowers fees faster than it raises mainnet throughput

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22206v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: crypto-structure, execution
  • Trading Relevance: 7
  • Time Horizon: cycle
  • Actionability: monitor
  • Henry Risk Link: liquidity, execution, regime
  • Why it matters: Ethereum’s transaction-cost story is improving materially, but the throughput story remains slower and uneven. The paper reports mainnet median fees falling from over 2tounder0.02 and L2 median fees falling more than 95% from 0.05to0.0015, yet mainnet TPS stays below 100 until 2034 in the forecast. This matters because lower fees can improve retail and arbitrage participation, but slow base-layer throughput still creates congestion risk under stress. Henry should separate cost compression from reliable exit capacity.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: From January 2024 through March 2026, Ethereum upgrades doubled TPS on both mainnet and L2 networks. Mainnet median fees fell from over 2tounder0.02, L2 median fees fell more than 95% to $0.0015, L2s are forecast to have lower median fees than Solana by October 2026, and L2s surpass Solana TPS in March 2029.
  • Cold read: The crypto infrastructure curve is becoming cheaper, but cheap is not the same as instantly scalable. Market structure can become more accessible while still fragile during correlated liquidation or bridge congestion.
  • Trading insight: Lower execution fees can increase strategy turnover and arbitrage intensity, narrowing some inefficiencies. But during stress, Henry should still price cross-chain latency, bridge risk, and finality uncertainty as separate slippage components.
  • Change sensor: L2 fee stability during volatility; blob expansion schedule; bridge congestion or sequencer downtime incidents.
  • Follow-up question: Which crypto venues improve execution quality from lower fees, and which only invite faster toxic flow?

[07:01 KST] Universal VaR superadditivity warns that diversification can fail under infinite-mean tails

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22884v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: risk-sizing
  • Trading Relevance: 9
  • Time Horizon: secular
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: sizing, leverage, correlation
  • Why it matters: Henry’s portfolio can look diversified by ticker while remaining concentrated in the same tail mechanism: BTC beta, MSTR reflexivity, leveraged crypto equity, and liquidity stress. This paper formalizes a more severe version of the classic VaR problem: for sufficiently heavy-tailed risks, portfolio VaR can become superadditive across all probability levels. In plain terms, adding positions can increase measured tail risk more than the sum of standalone risks. The lesson is not to abandon diversification, but to treat heavy-tail dependence and liquidity exits as the real unit of risk.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: The paper defines universal VaR superadditivity and weighted universal VaR superadditivity. It states that for any portfolio satisfying WUVS, every distortion risk measure is superadditive, so an optimal allocation concentrates on a single asset and diversification is never beneficial.
  • Cold read: In heavy-tail regimes, “many positions” may be a cosmetic hedge. If all positions need the same liquidity at the same time, portfolio construction can become risk multiplication.
  • Trading insight: Use this as a hard warning against full Kelly across correlated crypto wrappers. Position sizing should be based on shared liquidation channel and drawdown survival, not on normal covariance diversification.
  • Change sensor: Lower-tail dependence among BTC, MSTR, BMNR, miners, and crypto credit; rising funding stress; simultaneous ETF outflows and perp liquidations.
  • Follow-up question: What is Henry’s portfolio heat if all crypto-linked exposures are collapsed into one heavy-tail risk bucket?

[07:01 KST] Heavy-tailed Markov regimes can generate usable stress paths without overfitting complexity

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23492v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: risk-sizing, macro-liquidity
  • Trading Relevance: 8
  • Time Horizon: cycle
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: regime, sizing, correlation
  • Why it matters: Stress testing often fails because the model either memorizes history or assumes Gaussian returns that understate drawdowns. This paper argues that simple continuous hidden Markov models can work if the emission distributions are heavy-tailed, separating regime persistence from return-shape realism. That is directly useful for Henry because regime sizing needs scenarios beyond the one realized market path. It supports a practical middle ground: use interpretable regimes, but force fat-tailed emissions and conditional VaR checks.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: Across SPY walk-forward folds, a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel, CRSP cross-decade transfer, and a six-asset basket, heavy-tailed marginals closed most of the fit gap. The fitted model produced regime-conditional Value-at-Risk that passed a joint conditional-coverage test and a copula that reproduced cross-asset correlations.
  • Cold read: The model failure was distributional, not temporal. A few states may be enough, but the state return distributions must admit the tails that hurt real portfolios.
  • Trading insight: For ATLAS or manual pre-trade checks, model Base, Stress, and Crash regimes with Student-t or comparable heavy-tail emissions rather than more states for their own sake. The sizing output should be conditional VaR and drawdown survival, not only expected return.
  • Change sensor: Regime transition probabilities; conditional VaR breaches; whether realized correlations exceed the model copula during BTC drawdowns.
  • Follow-up question: Can Henry’s pre-trade checklist include a regime-conditional loss estimate for each planned add?

[07:01 KST] Factor models can pass the aggregate market while failing inside the market

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23596v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: capital-markets, risk-sizing
  • Trading Relevance: 7
  • Time Horizon: cycle
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: correlation, narrative, regime
  • Why it matters: Aggregate indexes can hide offsetting errors inside the market. The paper splits a CRSP investible market portfolio into size-ranked body and tail legs that recombine to the market; factor models pass the aggregate test but leave systematic offsetting alphas inside the body and tail. For Henry, this is a warning that “market is priced” does not mean each internal risk sleeve is priced. MSTR and BTC wrappers can live in a tail sleeve whose behavior is masked by the aggregate index.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: The paper finds that all models pass the aggregate market test. Yet q5 leaves systematic, offsetting alphas: negative in the body and positive in the tail. Random splits remove the rejection, suggesting the market can appear priced because internal pricing errors cancel.
  • Cold read: Index-level calm can be a netting illusion. Internal body-tail dispersion can matter more than the headline S&P or Nasdaq direction.
  • Trading insight: Do not let broad index confirmation override asset-specific structure. Henry should ask whether his positions sit in the body or tail of the current regime, and whether index-level factors are hiding local mispricing or fragility.
  • Change sensor: Size-ranked dispersion; small-cap and speculative-tail relative strength; MSTR versus BTC and Nasdaq residual behavior.
  • Follow-up question: Which aggregate indicators in Henry’s dashboard could be hiding offsetting internal stress?

[07:01 KST] Market randomness can be the residue of arbitrage pressure

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22743v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: microstructure, cognition
  • Trading Relevance: 8
  • Time Horizon: secular
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: FOMO, execution, regime
  • Why it matters: The paper starts from a smooth and predictable return path, then lets predictive traders exploit it until the market adapts against them. The result is not externally injected randomness but endogenous randomness: edge collapses toward zero and return sequences become balanced with low autocorrelation. For Henry, this is a direct antidote to chart-pattern overconfidence. A pattern can be real and still self-erasing once enough traders lean on it.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: In a medium-size experiment with in-sample length 2000 and out-of-sample length 10000, the final out-of-sample positive-return fraction is 0.5010, transition rate is 0.4896, and maximum absolute autocorrelation is 0.0275. The authors conclude that market randomness can arise as the endogenous residue of arbitrage pressure rather than exogenous stochastic shocks.
  • Cold read: Markets do not need outside news to destroy edge. Competition can turn predictability into noise through its own feedback loop.
  • Trading insight: Treat any simple signal as decaying capital. Increase friction, require out-of-sample validation, and reduce size when the signal becomes popular or too easy to explain.
  • Change sensor: Crowding in social narratives; degradation of post-entry markout; rising failed-breakout frequency after popular technical setups.
  • Follow-up question: How can Henry detect when an edge is being arbitraged away before P&L damage proves it?

[07:01 KST] IPO Finance Agent shows why long-document financial due diligence is a retrieval problem

  • URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23032v1
  • Source Type: paper
  • Domain: ai-world-sensing, capital-markets
  • Trading Relevance: 7
  • Time Horizon: secular
  • Actionability: study
  • Henry Risk Link: narrative, diligence, execution
  • Why it matters: Financial agents are moving from short periodic filings toward long and messy IPO documents, where naive chunk retrieval can fail outright. The paper reports that the original Finance Agent v2 harness basically failed to produce output on the SpaceX S-1 due to document length, requiring contextual retrieval and an improved evaluator-optimizer rubric process. For Henry, this is a warning against delegating capital decisions to agents that cannot prove retrieval coverage. It also matters because private-market and tokenized pre-IPO narratives are becoming tradeable before public-market scrutiny fully catches up.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: The authors introduce IPO Finance Agent, a benchmark with 1,000 IPO-diligence questions and 70 public SpaceX S-1 questions. Candidate facts are extracted from an ensemble of model answers, consolidated into draft criteria, audited for omissions, hallucinations, mistiered items, and redundancy, then repaired before human expert review.
  • Cold read: The bottleneck for financial AI is not only model IQ; it is evidence coverage and auditability. A confident answer without retrieval coverage is a hidden leverage point.
  • Trading insight: Any trading agent used for filings or capital-stack analysis should log source coverage, missed sections, contradiction checks, and rubric failures. Do not allow a financial agent to summarize a long filing without a retrieval audit.
  • Change sensor: Agent benchmarks moving from 10-K tasks to IPO and credit documents; evidence coverage metrics; tokenized private-market instruments that rely on thin disclosure digestion.
  • Follow-up question: What minimum retrieval-audit standard should ATLAS require before accepting an agent’s filing analysis?

[07:01 KST] AI data centers are becoming a grid-connection trade, not only a chip trade

  • Source Type: blog
  • Domain: ai-world-sensing, capital-markets
  • Trading Relevance: 8
  • Time Horizon: secular
  • Actionability: monitor
  • Henry Risk Link: narrative, regime, execution
  • Why it matters: The AI investment narrative still focuses on chips, models, and capex, but this source frames grid connectivity as the binding constraint. It notes compute used to train frontier models has been doubling every five to six months, while AI data centers have high power density, fast uncertain ramping, and low tolerance for interruption. This turns the AI trade into a physical infrastructure and permitting trade. Henry should treat AI upside as gated by power availability, grid queue, and operational flexibility rather than by GPU supply alone.
  • Raw excerpt / abstract: WEF states that investment in AI data centres is growing faster than power grids can keep up, making grid connectivity a constraint. DNV estimates that by 2060 roughly 80% of data center electricity demand will come from AI and the sector reaches 11% of final electricity demand. The article says grid-connection uncertainty now rivals technology risk and that AI data centers combine high power density with fast uncertain ramping and low tolerance for interruptibility.
  • Cold read: The AI boom is becoming a power-delivery bottleneck. Capital and chips can be ordered faster than transmission, interconnection, permitting, and reliability rules can adapt.
  • Trading insight: For AI-linked equities, distinguish companies with secured power, grid access, and behind-the-meter options from companies only announcing capacity. For macro, AI capex may keep nominal investment high while execution bottlenecks delay revenue realization.
  • Change sensor: Grid interconnection queue length; data-center curtailment contracts; hyperscaler power purchase agreements and on-site generation announcements.
  • Follow-up question: Which AI infrastructure names have actual deliverable power versus paper capacity?
