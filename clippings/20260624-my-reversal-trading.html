Lesson 1: What Is Mean Reversion?

Before learning the strategy, you need to understand what mean reversion trading actually is and how it differs from the other main trading style.

Remember this:

Every filter, entry trigger, and risk rule in this article is built on one core idea:

You are betting against momentum traders when they’re in a weak position.

But what is a momentum trader?

The Two Main Trading Styles

There are two fundamental ways to trade:

  1. Momentum Trading

Momentum traders bet that the price will keep moving in the same direction.

They’re also known as breakout traders.

If price breaks through a support level (a price zone where buying pressure previously stopped the decline), momentum traders short the breakdown, expecting the price to keep running lower.

  1. Mean Reversion Trading

Mean reversion traders bet that the price will reverse direction.

For example:

If the price spikes up quickly, they sell, expecting it to fall back down.

If the price approaches a support level, mean reversion traders go long at the level, expecting the price to bounce back up.

A momentum trader sees price approaching support and thinks:

“This level is about to break. I’ll short the breakdown.”

But a mean reversion trader sees the same chart and thinks:

“This level is about to hold. I’ll long the reversal.”

Only one of them can be right, and this isn’t decided by the chart.

It’s decided by the assumption about what the level will do…

Mean Reversion = Counter-Trading Momentum Traders

Here’s the key insight that changes how you think about mean reversion:

Every mean reversion trade is a bet against someone else.

When you short a spike into resistance, you’re betting against the traders who just bought that spike and expecting a breakout above.

When you go long after a dump into support, you’re betting against the traders who just shorted and expecting continuation down.

You are counter-trading momentum traders.

This reframe is important.

Instead of asking “will this level hold? ”, you can finally visualise who you’re trading against and ask:

“Are the momentum traders in a weak position? ”🤔

When you take mean reversion trades:

If momentum traders are weak, your mean reversion trade is more likely to work.

If momentum traders are strong, your mean reversion trade will probably fail.

I’ll teach you exactly how to identify when momentum traders are weak in the next lesson (I use a simple 4-filter checklist)

Lesson 1 Summary🎓

There are two main trading styles: momentum (betting price continues) and mean reversion (betting price reverses)

Mean reversion trading is about counter-trading momentum traders

You profit when momentum traders are in a weak position

Lesson 2: Optimal Trade Environment

Understanding that you’re counter-trading momentum traders is now your foundation.

But you also need to know when they’re actually vulnerable.

To answer this, use this framework:

4-Filter Checklist for Mean Reversion Trades

Filter 1: How Did Price Approach the Level?

Of all the variables, this is the most important one.

I’ve analysed 600+ trades where fast spikes yield a 70% win rate for mean reversion trades vs only 30% for momentum trades.

This is the most skewed variable in the entire dataset.

What you WANT to see:✅

A fast vertical spike into resistance

Price shoots up in one or two large candles

This shows exhaustion (not sustainable momentum)

After a spike, buyers’ strength is depleted, and price typically reverses

What you DON’T want to see:❌

A slow, grinding staircase pattern approaching resistance

Each candle makes incremental progress

Higher lows are stacking up

This is a controlled, deliberate movement (bad for mean reversion)

Real Trade Example:

Here we have a fast spike into the level and also a big volume spike (more on this later).

The fast spike pattern shows sellers have exhausted themselves in a rush to get out.

When this hits support, there’s no one left to push lower and price reverses.

Think of it this way🤔:

A fast spike is like a sprinter who goes all-out in the first 50 meters of a 100-meter race.

By the time they hit the finish line (support), they’re gasping for air.

But a slow staircase is like a marathon runner pacing themselves. They still have energy when they reach the level.

These fast spikes can be broken down into two components:

Slope (how steep the move is)

Size (how large the move is relative to recent price action)

Common mistake: Traders see a strong candle hit resistance and assume momentum is strong, but these fast moves often signal exhaustion, not strength.

Do this instead: Take mean reversion trades when price SPIKES into resistance in one or two candles, not when it grinds slowly.

Filter 1: fast vertical spike ✅

If you are unclear on reading price action and don’t understand what I mean by the ‘staircase’ pattern, check out my price action masterclass at the end of this article.

Filter 2: What Did Volume Look Like?

Volume tells you whether the move into the level has real participation behind it, or whether market participants are exhausted.

This matters because mean reversion also works when the move is exhausted.

If fresh buyers keep arriving (increasing volume), the move has fuel left, and the level is more likely to break.

If volume is flat or declining, the move is losing steam.

What you WANT to see:✅

Flat or decreasing volume as price approaches resistance

This pattern shows the move lacks sustained participation

What you DON’T want to see:❌

Gradual increase in volume (this is good for momentum, bad for mean reversion)

Increasing volume means fresh buyers keep arriving

Remember: Mean reversion traders are counter-trading momentum traders. Every variable that hurts momentum helps you.

Real Trade Example:

How to tell if volume is flat or increasing:

Draw an imaginary line through the average volume. If the slope looks flat, it’s flat volume (good for MR).

If the slope clearly angles upward, it’s increasing volume (bad for MR).

Common mistake: Volume that increases DURING the spike creation can be misleading. This is “spike-induced volume” caused by one participant (or a small group) spamming market buys.

Do this instead: Look at the volume BEFORE the spike began. If it was flat before and only increased during the spike itself, you can dismiss that increase.

Filter 1: fast vertical spike ✅

Filter 2: flat or decreasing volume ✅

If you want to learn more about volume, use my volume masterclass at the end of this article under Bonus Materials.

Filter 3: Moving Average Crossovers

The first two filters tell you about the spike itself.

But this filter zooms out and asks: What kind of market are you in?

Mean reversion thrives in choppy, indecisive markets where price keeps changing direction and levels hold (instead of trending).

But the problem is that “choppy” and “trending” are subjective:

Two traders can look at the same chart and disagree, so you need a way to measure it.

A moving average solves this

This filter helps distinguish choppy markets (good for mean reversion) from trending markets (bad for mean reversion).

BTW, I’ll cover them briefly now, but I have an in-depth guide on moving averages that I released recently.

How Many Moving Average Crossovers should you look for?

The noise classification combines two measurements:

How many times price crosses the 30MA

Whether the moving average direction is trending or sideways

For mean reversion, here’s what you WANT to see✅:

Frequent crossovers (price keeps changing direction)

Sideways moving average direction

👉This indicates chop and indecision, and mean reversion thrives in choppy conditions because levels hold

What you DON’T want to see❌:

Minimal crossovers (price stays on one side of the MA)

Trending moving average direction

👉This signals a directional trend, and mean reversion trades fail in trending conditions because levels break.

Remember: More crossovers with a sideways MA = choppier market = better mean reversion conditions.

Real Trade Example:

Think of it this way🤔: Frequent MA crossovers mean price keeps changing direction. It’s like a tennis ball bouncing between the floor and the ceiling. That’s exactly what mean reversion bets on.

Tips to identify choppy price action:

Price rejecting nearby levels 3+ times

Price slicing through the MAs frequently (many crossovers)

Support and resistance levels being respected, not broken

Filter 1: fast vertical spike ✅

Filter 2: flat or decreasing volume ✅

Filter 3: frequent MA crossovers ✅

Filter 4: What Does the Left-Hand Side Price Action Look Like?

This filter examines the previous 4-8 hours of price action before your entry.

When you take a trade, you’re betting that the conditions on the left (before entry) will continue on the right (after entry):

If the left side was choppy with levels holding, you expect the same after entry.

If the left side was trending with levels breaking, you should expect more of the same.

What you WANT to see✅:

Choppy, sideways price action

Price rejecting nearby levels multiple times

Support and resistance levels are being respected, not broken

Moving averages criss-crossing frequently (not fanned out)

What you DON’T want to see❌:

A clean trending staircase (good for momentum, bad for mean reversion)

Price consistently breaking through levels without rejection

Moving averages fanned out in one direction for an extended period

Think of it this way🤔:

If you imagine taking momentum trades on the left-hand side, would they have worked?

If momentum trades had been “in sync” (winning consistently), it’s a trending environment and bad for mean reversion.

If momentum trades had been “out of sync” (losing consistently), it’s a choppy environment and good for mean reversion.

Real Trade Example:

The nuance: Sometimes price action can “look like” a grindy staircase, but it’s actually a “choppy range that’s slanted.”

The key difference:

Choppy range (slanted): Nearby levels are being rejected 3+ times, MAs are criss-crossing, and breakouts keep failing

True staircase: Levels break cleanly, MAs are fanned out, momentum trades would have been “in sync.”

Common mistake: Traders see price moving generally upward and assume it’s a trend. A slanted choppy range looks similar but yields very different results.

Do this instead: Check if nearby support/resistance levels are being respected or broken. If they’re being respected (multiple rejections), it’s choppy. If they’re breaking cleanly, it’s trending.

Filter 1: fast vertical spike ✅

Filter 2: flat or decreasing volume ✅

Filter 3: frequent MA crossovers ✅

Filter 4: choppy/sideways left-hand side ✅

Lesson 3: Strategy Logic: Drawing Levels and Identifying Setup

Now that know what mean reversion is and when conditions favor it,

You need to know where to look for trades and how to execute them mechanically.

Mean reversion trades happen at key price levels, so you need a consistent method for identifying those levels.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to draw support and resistance levels consistently, identify your entry trigger on the 1-minute chart, and confirm whether a setup aligns with optimal mean reversion conditions.

Step 1: Draw Support and Resistance Levels

Support and resistance are price zones where buyers or sellers previously showed interest.

These levels represent points where significant order flow caused price to reverse. When price returns to these zones, the same dynamic can repeat.

Mean reversion trades happen at these key levels. You need to identify them consistently.

Common mistake: Traders draw levels randomly or inconsistently, leading to missed setups or unreliable data.

Do this instead: Use the same four-level process every single time.

For a deeper dive into level selection, including structural methods and level quality assessment, check out the support and resistance masterclass linked at the end of this article.

Step 2: Await Your Entry Trigger on the 1-Minute Chart

Once you’ve identified your levels, switch to the 1-minute chart for precise entry timing.

The 1-minute chart allows you to spot your entry trigger:

👉1 candle close back below (or above) the level after the spike.

Here’s the sequence for a mean reversion short at resistance:

Price spikes up into your resistance level

Price trades above the level briefly

You wait for one complete 1-minute candle to close back below the resistance level

That candle close is your entry trigger

For a mean reversion long at support, the sequence is reversed:

Price spikes down through support, then you wait for one candle to close back above the support level.

Step 3: Aligning Setups with Optimal Mean Reversion Conditions

Before entering a trade, check the four filters from Lesson 2:

Did price SPIKE into the level (not grind)?

Is volume flat or decreasing (not gradually increasing)?

Are there frequent MA crossovers (choppy, not trending)?

Is the left-hand side choppy/sideways (not clean trending)?

If any filter fails, reduce your risk on the trade.

Only take full risk on A-grade setups, not forcing trades in poor conditions.

Lesson 4: Strategy Logic- Stop Loss and Take Profit

You’ve drawn your levels, spotted your entry trigger, and confirmed the setup aligns with optimal mean reversion conditions.

Now you need to know exactly where to place your stop loss and take profit so that each trade has defined risk.

Without precise placement rules, you’ll either set stops too tight (getting chopped out by normal volatility) or too wide (losing too much on a single trade).

Step 4: Stop Loss at the Previous Swing High (Minimum 1%)

A swing high is the highest wick in a local push up, followed by price moving away (it failed to make higher highs).

Every “inverted V” shape on the chart represents a swing high.

For mean reversion longs, the equivalent is a swing low:

The lowest wick in a local push down, followed by price moving away.

Real Trade Example:

Minimum 1% rule: If the swing high is less than 1% away from your entry, skip it and use the next swing high up. Stops that are too tight get hit by normal price noise. You need at least 1% breathing room for the trade to work.

Example: You enter at 100.Thenearestswinghighis100.50 (0.5% away). Skip it. Use the next swing high at $101.50 (1.5% away).

This becomes your stop.

Avoid this common mistake: Traders place stops at the nearest swing high even if it’s only 0.3% away, leading to frequent stop-outs from normal volatility.

Do this instead: Always measure the distance. If it’s less than 1%, use the next swing high up.

Step 5: Take Profit at 1R (Equal Distance to Stop)

Your take profit target is 1R: the same distance as your stop loss, but in the profit direction.

1R means your risk-to-reward ratio is 1:1. If you’re risking 1.50toyourstop,yourtargetis1.50 of profit.

Here’s how to set it:

Select the ‘Long tool’ on TradingView

Drag your stop loss level to your entry

Drag your Take Profit to ‘1R’. It will clearly say “Risk/Reward Ratio: 1”

Why 1R?

It’s conservative and achievable. Mean reversion trades often hit 1R quickly because exhausted moves reverse fast.

You’re not trying to catch the entire reversal. You’re simply capturing a high-probability piece of it.

Remember, this foundational mean reversion framework is designed to build consistency, not maximise edge.

Real Trade Example:

Lesson 4 Summary🎓

Place your stop loss at the most recent swing high (for shorts) or swing low (for longs), minimum 1% away from entry

Set your take profit at 1R: equal distance to your stop loss, in the profit direction

If the nearest swing point is less than 1% away, skip it and use the next one
